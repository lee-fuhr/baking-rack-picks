How to choose the right baking rack

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Look for a stainless steel baking rack with a tight grid pattern and tall feet for even airflow and stable cooling. Prioritize dishwasher safety and a size that fits your sheet pans, but skip non-stick coatings if you want the longest-lasting rack.

What size baking rack should I buy?

Start with the pan you already own. A rack that sits inside a half-sheet pan (roughly 18 by 13 inches) is the most versatile size for cookies, rolls, and roasting. If you bake a lot of cakes or round loaves, a round rack that fits your standard cake pan is just as handy. Keep in mind that oversized racks that overhang the pan can tip or drip onto the oven floor, while undersized ones waste pan space. Measure your pans before you shop, and look for racks described as fitting standard quarter-sheet, half-sheet, or full-sheet pans.

Stainless steel vs. non-stick: which coating lasts longer?

Plain stainless steel racks are the workhorses of most professional kitchens. They won’t chip, flake, or wear down over time, and they can handle high roasting temperatures without worry. The trade-off is that sticky glazes or sugar-heavy pastries can be a pain to clean off. Non-stick coated racks make cleanup noticeably easier, especially for candied nuts or sticky buns. However, the coating can scratch if you use metal utensils or abrasive scrubbers, and it may degrade after a year or two of frequent use. If you want a rack that will outlast your mixer, go bare stainless. If you value quick cleanup over longevity, a quality non-stick rack is a fair compromise.

What does grid pattern and wire spacing matter for cooling?

A tight grid, with wires spaced roughly half an inch apart, supports delicate cookies and smaller pastries so they don’t sag through the gaps. It also works well for cooling a batch of cinnamon rolls or scones without leaving deep wire marks in the bottoms. Coarser grids (wires an inch or more apart) are better for large items like whole roasts, bundt cakes, or bread loaves, but they let small cookies and pie crusts fall through. Two racks with different spacing give you the most flexibility, but if you can only buy one, a medium-to-tight grid (about 3/8 to 1/2 inch spacing) is the safest all-rounder.

How high should the feet be on a cooling rack?

Taller feet, around an inch or more, allow air to circulate freely under the food, which prevents soggy bottoms on fried chicken or glazed doughnuts. They also keep the rack stable above the pan so drips don’t pool around the food. Shorter feet (half an inch or less) make the rack nest more compactly for storage and still work fine for cookies or bread that you aren’t glazing. If you plan to use the rack inside a roasting pan for drip-catching, look for feet that lift the rack high enough that the food never sits in its own juices.

Can I put a baking rack in the dishwasher?

Many stainless steel racks are labeled dishwasher-safe, and they hold up well if you avoid harsh drying cycles. However, the high heat and detergent can eventually dull the finish and make food stick more over time. Non-stick racks often say dishwasher-safe too, but the coating tends to break down faster with repeated dishwasher use. For the longest life on any rack, hand-washing with a soft sponge and mild soap is the best practice. If you rely on the dishwasher, expect to replace a non-stick rack sooner than a plain stainless model.

Frequently asked questions

Is a baking rack the same as a cooling rack?

Yes. The terms are used interchangeably. A baking rack is designed for both cooling baked goods and for elevating food while roasting or glazing.

Can I use a baking rack in the oven?

Most metal baking racks are oven-safe up to 400°F or higher, but always check the manufacturer’s recommendation. Non-stick coatings may have lower heat limits, so avoid using them under the broiler.

How many baking racks do I really need?

Two standard half-sheet-size racks are enough for most home bakers. One lets you cool a batch while the other is in the oven or being washed. If you bake frequently or in large batches, three or four will keep your workflow smooth.

Do I need a rack with silicone feet or handles?

Silicone feet prevent the rack from scratching your pans and countertops, and they add a little extra stability. They are a nice bonus but not essential. Silicone handles make lifting a hot rack safer, though most racks are thin enough to grab with a mitt anyway.

Why does my rack rust after a few washes?

Rust usually appears on racks that are plated with chrome or have a low-grade stainless steel mix. Pure stainless steel (often labeled 18/8 or 304) is highly rust-resistant. If your rack rusts, it’s likely made from lesser stainless or has a scratched plating. Dry it immediately after washing to slow new spots.

Can I stack racks on top of each other for storage?

Yes, many racks are designed to nest or stack. Look for models that have a small lip or dimples on the feet so they interlock without wobbling. Stacking saves space, but avoid cramming them together so tightly that they scratch each other’s surfaces.

In shortA good baking rack is a simple tool that makes a real difference in your results, from crisper roast chicken to cookies that cool without steaming. Focus on stainless steel, a grid that matches the sizes you bake most, and feet tall enough to let air move freely. Buy one that fits your pans and your dishwasher habits, and it will serve you well for years.
